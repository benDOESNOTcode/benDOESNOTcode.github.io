Toxic Tides: Impact of Microplastic on the Plankton Population

We’ve all seen harrowing images: ducks entangled in plastic, turtles mistaking plastic bags for jellyfish, and large fish like tuna found with plastic in their stomachs. A staggering study revealed that in 2010, an estimated 4.8 to 12.7 million metric tons of plastic entered the oceans. But what about now, a decade and a half later? Have we made progress, or has the problem worsened?

Our Study

Our study delves into a critical aspect of this crisis: the impact of microplastics on phytoplankton. We aim to uncover whether a significant relationship exists between the two and to what extent this issue has escalated.

Distribution of Plankton

Similar to the plastic dataset, we divided the phytoplankton dataset into three time periods: 1958–2000, 2001–2010, and 2011–2017. During the latter half of the 20th century, phytoplankton populations were thriving along the Indian coast and the American coast, with densities reaching approximately 1 million mg per m².

Statistical Analysis

The graph shows a sharp increase in microplastic levels starting from 2011, while plankton densities exhibit a continuous decline over the same timeframe. This contrasting trend suggests an inverse relationship, with rising microplastic pollution potentially contributing to the depletion of plankton populations. But is this really true? Thats why we used three seperate test to cofiirm our results.

Figure 10: Microplastic vs Plankton Line Plot.

To understand the relationship between microplastic pollution and plankton populations, we employed three statistical techniques: Spearman Correlation, Pearson Correlation, and the Chi-Square Test. Each method offers unique insights into the nature of the relationship between these variables.

  • Spearman Correlation: This non-parametric method assesses the strength and direction of the monotonic relationship between two variables. It is particularly useful when the data may not follow a normal distribution. In our analysis, the Spearman correlation coefficient was -0.13 (rounded), with a p-value of 0.53. This weak negative correlation suggests that as microplastic levels increase, plankton densities tend to decrease, but the relationship is not statistically significant.
  • Pearson Correlation: The Pearson correlation measures the linear relationship between two variables. It assumes that the data is normally distributed and quantifies how closely the variables vary together. Our Pearson correlation coefficient was -0.13 (rounded), with a p-value of 0.56, indicating a weak negative linear relationship. However, like the Spearman result, this correlation is not statistically significant.
  • Chi-Square Test: The Chi-Square test is used to determine whether there is a significant association between categorical variables. Although not designed for continuous data, it can provide additional context for the relationship. Our test statistic was 0.44 (rounded), with a p-value of 0.80, suggesting no significant association between microplastic pollution and plankton populations.

Final Thoughts and Conclusion

These limitations underscore the challenges of relying on incomplete and regionally skewed datasets to understand the full scope of environmental damage. The cessation of direct data collection in 1994 and the reliance on satellite imagery limit the depth and accuracy of current analyses, potentially overlooking critical changes beneath the ocean’s surface. Moreover, the dataset’s focus on the United States after 2000 fails to capture the ecological impacts in other regions, such as the waters around India, which are home to diverse and vital marine ecosystems. However, even with these gaps, the evidence of human impact on the oceans is undeniable. From skyrocketing plastic pollution to fish suffering from reproductive failure and respiratory issues, the signs of distress are clear. Addressing these challenges requires both better data and immediate action to reduce pollution and protect marine life. The urgency to act has never been greater, and the responsibility lies with all of us.
